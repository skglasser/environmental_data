Q3:

Based on both the histograms and Shapiro normality tests, the unconditioned body masses and body length are definitely not normally-distributed. For both the Shapiro test the p value is <0.05 meaning that we can reject the null hypothesis that the data points are normally distributed. This is corroborated with the visualization of the histograms that both show evidence of right skew.

Q18

The AIC measures the goodness of fit for a model, it also penalizes the number of parameters included in the model. The AIC is a helpful tool for choosing the most parsimonious model because just by comparing the model fit based on the R^2 values could be misleading. The R^2 value generally is generally closer to 1, better fit, with more model parameters. The more parameterized the model the more complicated to explain and understand. Based on the AIC and taking in mind the trade off between more parameters and more model parsimony, I would choose the additive model because the AIC is slightly smaller and it is more simple than the interaction model. With more predictor terms in the model the more complex it is. While the interaction between sex and species is interesting it does not appear to have a significant effect on body mass and would be better left as single predictors in the additive model.
